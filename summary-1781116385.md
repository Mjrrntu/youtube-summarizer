**# Main Topic**  
Jim Keller’s Tenstor project: an open‑source AI inference chip that eliminates traditional GPU schedulers, uses GDDR6 memory, and relies on compiler‑managed data movement to match Nvidia performance while costing five times less.

**# Key Points**

- **Foundational Decision** – Keller rejected all Nvidia assumptions, starting the design from scratch.  
- **GPU Inefficiency** – Less than 50 % of GPU silicon performs math; the rest handles scheduling, caching, and memory management.  
- **Predictable AI Workloads** – Because data movement is deterministic, dedicated hardware schedulers are unnecessary.  
- **Hardware Simplification** – All schedulers and traffic controllers were removed; the compiler pre‑maps every data movement before execution.  
- **Core Architecture** – 352 Tensix cores, each with its own local SRAM; no global clock or resource contention.  
- **Memory Choice** – Uses inexpensive GDDR6 instead of HBM; compiler pre‑fetches from GDDR6 into SRAM so low bandwidth rarely hurts.  
- **Scaling Across Chips** – Each chip contains 400 GB/s Ethernet; software pre‑maps inter‑chip data movement, treating many chips as one unified brain.  
- **Performance & Cost** – Benchmarks show ~350 tokens/s on DeepSeek R1, costing $6 per million tokens versus Nvidia’s $30.  
- **Software Ecosystem** – Fully open‑source compiler; 90 % of Hugging Face models run “out of the box,” but 10 % require tuning.  
- **Maturity Gap** – Black‑hole software is newer than previous Tenstor generations; enterprise buyers demand full compatibility.  
- **Leadership Pattern** – Keller’s history of building foundations and then leaving projects (Zen, Apple A‑series, Tesla) suggests he may depart again.  
- **Other Competitors** – A separate unnamed company also threatened Nvidia, which paid $20 B to acquire it.

**# Conclusions**

Tenstor’s chip architecture offers comparable inference performance to Nvidia at a fraction of the cost by removing scheduler overhead and using compiler‑managed data movement. However, the software stack is still catching up, and Keller’s tendency to move on may delay full adoption in large data‑center deployments.

**# Practical Takeaways**

- **Cost‑Effective Inference** – Evaluate Tenstor chips for projects where inference cost is critical and current GPU pricing is a bottleneck.  
- **Software Compatibility** – Test models against the open‑source compiler; be prepared for the 10 % that may need manual adjustments.  
- **Future Upgrades** – Expect rapid improvements from the open‑source community; monitor updates to the compiler and hardware drivers.  
- **Risk Assessment** – For mission‑critical applications (e.g., medical imaging, finance), weigh the current maturity gap against potential cost savings.  

**# TL;DR**

Jim Keller’s Tenstor built a cheap, open‑source AI inference chip that removes GPU schedulers, uses GDDR6 memory, and relies on compiler‑managed data movement. It matches Nvidia’s performance but costs five times less, yet its software is still maturing and Keller may leave soon, so widespread data‑center adoption remains uncertain.
